# TECH: UX process — Define (Phase 2)

## What it does

The second of five UX-process phases. CRITICAL priority. Phase 2 converts research findings from Phase 1 into concrete artifacts that drive design decisions: personas, journey maps, problem statements, and a prioritized feature list.

## When to use

Immediately after Phase 1 (Discover & Research) produces interview transcripts, analytics insights, and pain-point clusters. Run Phase 2 before any wireframes or mockups exist — without a persona and a journey map, Phase 3 (Ideate) produces decoration, not solutions.

## How it works

Four deliverables:

- **User personas** — 3-5 archetypes grounded in interview data (not brainstorming). Each: name + photo, goals, pain points, behaviors, context, representative quote. Referred to by name in all subsequent design reviews.
- **Journey maps** — end-to-end flow through the product or problem space. Steps, emotions (frustration / confidence / delight), touch-points, drop-off points.
- **Problem statements** — "How Might We" framing, one per top pain point. E.g. "How might we help Sarah order groceries in under 10 minutes during her commute?"
- **Feature prioritization** — impact × feasibility matrix. Rank all candidate features; ship the top 3-5.

## Minimal example

Persona extracted from 8 interviews:

```markdown
## Sarah, The Busy Parent
Age 34 · Marketing Manager · Austin, TX

### Goals
- Order groceries in under 10 min during her commute
- Never run out of diapers, milk, snacks

### Pain Points
- Scrolling past irrelevant promos to find basics
- Forgetting items because reorder is hidden

### Behaviors
- Shops on phone, never desktop
- Reorders 70% of the same items weekly

> "I just need the basics fast. Don't make me think about groceries more than I have to."
```

How-Might-We:

> "How might we let Sarah reorder her weekly basics in under 90 seconds from a mobile screen?"

## Gotchas

- Aspirational personas ("our user is innovative and tech-savvy") without research backing are worse than no personas — they codify bias.
- HMW statements should be narrow enough to inspire a solution but not so narrow they specify one ("How might we add a 'reorder' button" is a solution, not a problem).
- Journey maps need the emotional axis (high / low / neutral) — without it, they degenerate into flowcharts and lose the "where do we feel bad" signal.
- Feature prioritization without a constraint (ship top 3 by Q2) turns into everything-is-P1.
